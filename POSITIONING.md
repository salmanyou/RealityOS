# Positioning — lead with the pain

Customers don't buy RealityOS. They buy outcomes. RealityOS is the engine underneath — name it the way GitHub names Git: rarely, and only to people who care how it works.

## The one rule
**Lead with the pain. The engine is the footnote.**
- AWS sells "run your app reliably," not distributed-systems theory.
- Stripe sells "accept payments in minutes," not payment protocols.
- GitHub sold "host and collaborate on code," not Git internals.

## What customers actually buy (lead with these)
- Earlier blocker detection — *know what's about to slip before standup*
- Less firefighting — *problems arrive already surfaced, with owners*
- Better delivery predictability — *a release date you can trust*
- Fewer missed deadlines
- Shorter status meetings

## Say this / not that
| Persona | Say (outcome) | Not (engine) |
|---|---|---|
| Eng lead | "Catch blockers before they cost you a day." | "Event-sourced reality model with inference." |
| EM / Director | "Predict slips early enough to act." | "RealityQL over a kernel with RFCs." |
| Founder/CTO | "Stop losing releases to things nobody flagged." | "The Organizational Reality Model." |

The product name customers see is **Blocker Radar**. "RealityOS" appears only in developer docs, the API, and conversations with people who ask "how does it work?"

## The milestone order (don't skip)
```
1. One team installs it
2. They keep using it
3. They recommend it
4. They pay
5. They ask for another feature
   → only NOW build RealityOS v1.1
```
Version 1.1 comes **after** payment, driven by what a paying team asks for — not before.

## The shift to aim for
Stop saying *"RealityOS can…"*. Start saying *"It has already…"* — backed by `Evidence.md`. Every real result you log there is one more sentence you can say in the present tense, which is worth more than any feature.
